## Abstract

This document develops a formal mathematical companion to the empirical PETU atlas.
It formalizes the hypothesis that coherent natural systems may often be described by a role-equivalent architecture involving support $(H)$, structure $(S)$ and linkage $(L)$, with system-level coherence or functional closure denoted by $\Phi$.
The theory does not assume that different natural domains share identical mechanisms.
Instead, it defines a common structural grammar: support becomes organized into structure, structure enables linkage, and linkage contributes to closure at the system level.
The document introduces normalized triadic states, admissible systems, unity, balance, dominance, fragility, co-inherence, triadic gain, non-circularity requirements, perturbative response, multi-scale closure, categorical comparison, tensorial extension, variational stability and law-candidate criteria.
It also formalizes the distinction between raw support $\Hraw$ and structured support $\Hstr$, which is necessary for interpreting apparent support-only effects.

## Admissible systems and observation domains

Let $X$ be a natural system and $\Omega_X$ its observational domain.
A measured state is indexed by $\omega\in\Omega_X$.
A PETU-admissible system belongs to a class $\Nstar$ if there exist declared, measurable and normalized functions
\[
H_X,S_X,L_X,\Phi_X:\Omega_X\rightarrow[0,1],
\]
such that $H_X$, $S_X$ and $L_X$ preserve their structural roles and $\Phi_X$ is either external, derived with declared construction, or explicitly labelled as an internal closure endpoint.

The triadic state is
\[
T_X(\omega)=\big(H_X(\omega),S_X(\omega),L_X(\omega)\big).
\]
For dynamic systems, $\omega$ may include time:
\[
\omega=(i,t),
\]
where $i$ denotes a unit and $t$ denotes time.

## Structural-role functions

### Support

Support is the enabling substrate or capacity of the system:
\[
H_X=\text{support function of }X.
\]
Depending on the domain, it may represent energy, mass, abundance, signal capacity, flow magnitude, resource availability, density, amplitude, reaction capacity or substrate potential.

### Structure

Structure is support under constraint:
\[
S_X=\text{structure function of }X.
\]
It may represent topology, modularity, shape, hierarchy, order, spatial organization, temporal recurrence, folding, pattern, network form or internal constraint.

### Linkage

Linkage is structure in relation:
\[
L_X=\text{linkage function of }X.
\]
It may represent coupling, interaction, synchrony, transfer, binding, flow, connectivity, relational continuity, teleconnection, co-regulation or integration.

### Closure

Closure is the degree to which support, structure and linkage cohere as an organized whole:
\[
\Phi_X=\text{closure function of }X.
\]
Depending on the domain, $\Phi_X$ may represent coherence, unity, resilience, integration, functional stability, system-level order, global organization or natural-state integrity.

## Raw support and structured support

A central methodological distinction is
\[
\Hraw \neq \Hstr.
\]
Raw support is minimally organized support:
\[
\Hraw=\text{support prior to explicit structural inscription}.
\]
Structured support is support already containing structural information:
\[
\Hstr=\Hraw\oplus S,
\]
where $\oplus$ denotes a domain-specific operation of structural inscription.

Therefore, a model
\[
H\rightarrow L
\]
does not necessarily imply that pure support generates linkage. If $H=\Hstr$, then
\[
H\rightarrow L
\quad\text{may mean}\quad
(\Hraw\oplus S)\rightarrow L.
\]
The clean emergence test is therefore
\[
\Hraw+S\rightarrow L
\quad\text{versus}\quad
\Hraw\rightarrow L.
\]
A high-performance $H\rightarrow L$ result is evidence for monadic support only if $H$ has been shown to be genuinely raw.

## Mathematical assumptions

The formal theory assumes:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $H_X,S_X,L_X,\Phi_X\in[0,1]$ after declared normalization.
\item The variables are role-equivalent, not physically identical across domains.
\item The normalization method, observation units, grouping structure and scale parameters are declared.
\item $\Phi_X$ is protected against circularity by endpoint labelling and anti-leakage checks.
\item Performance functionals $P$ are declared before confirmatory testing.
\item Group-aware validation is required when observations are nested by subject, site, region, model, scale or time.
\item Perturbations are auxiliary stress-tests unless the perturbation is independently justified as domain-relevant.
\end{enumerate}

## Unity, balance, dominance and fragility

### Unity

The minimal unity operator is the geometric mean:
\[
U_X=(H_XS_XL_X)^{1/3}.
\]
If any essential component collapses, unity collapses:
\[
H_X=0\Rightarrow U_X=0,
\qquad
S_X=0\Rightarrow U_X=0,
\qquad
L_X=0\Rightarrow U_X=0.
\]
Unity is therefore conjunctive rather than additive.

### Balance

Because $H_X,S_X,L_X\in[0,1]$, define
\[
B_X=
1-
\left(
\max\{H_X,S_X,L_X\}
-
\min\{H_X,S_X,L_X\}
\right).
\]
Then $B_X\in[0,1]$.
If the three components are equal, $B_X=1$.
If one component is maximal and another minimal, $B_X$ approaches $0$.

### Dominance

Dominance must be defined unambiguously. Two useful variants are:
\[
D_X^{gap}=
\max\{H_X,S_X,L_X\}
-
\operatorname{median}\{H_X,S_X,L_X\},
\]
and
\[
D_X^{share}=
\frac{\max\{H_X,S_X,L_X\}}
{H_X+S_X+L_X+\varepsilon},
\qquad \varepsilon>0.
\]
$D_X^{gap}$ measures excess over the median, while $D_X^{share}$ measures capture of the total triadic mass.

### Fragility

A minimal fragility operator is
\[
F_X=1-U_XB_X.
\]
A richer form is
\[
F_X=
\alpha(1-U_X)
+
\beta(1-B_X)
+
\gamma D_X^{gap}
+
\delta R_X,
\qquad
\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta\geq0.
\]
Here $R_X$ denotes rupture, discontinuity or instability.
Fragility is relational: it is not merely the absence of one component, but the failure of coherent relation among the components.

## Closure functions and triadic gain

The general closure relation is
\[
\Phi_X\approx\mathcal{F}_X(H_X,S_X,L_X).
\]
A minimal interpretable statistical model is
\[
\Phi_X =
\beta_0+\beta_HH_X+\beta_SS_X+\beta_LL_X
+\beta_{HS}H_XS_X+\beta_{HL}H_XL_X+\beta_{SL}S_XL_X
+\beta_{HSL}H_XS_XL_X+\varepsilon_X.
\]
The triadic interaction term is $\beta_{HSL}H_XS_XL_X$.
However, PETU does not require the true closure function to be polynomial.
$\mathcal{F}_X$ may be nonlinear, dynamical, graph-based, information-theoretic or topological.

Let
\[
\mathcal{M}_1=\{H,S,L\},\qquad
\mathcal{M}_2=\{H+S,H+L,S+L\},\qquad
\mathcal{M}_3=\{H+S+L\}.
\]
Let $P(\mathcal{M})$ be a declared performance functional.
The primary difference-form triadic gain is
\[
G_3^{diff}
=
P(\mathcal{M}_3)
-
\max_{\mathcal{M}\in\mathcal{M}_1\cup\mathcal{M}_2}P(\mathcal{M}).
\]
A ratio form may also be reported:
\[
G_3^{ratio}
=
\frac{P(\mathcal{M}_3)}
{\max_{\mathcal{M}\in\mathcal{M}_1\cup\mathcal{M}_2}P(\mathcal{M})+\varepsilon}.
\]
Triadic gain is strong when
\[
G_3^{diff}>\tau_G
\]
for a preregistered threshold $\tau_G$.

## Non-circularity and endpoint independence

The main methodological risk is circular closure: constructing $\Phi_X$ directly from $H_X,S_X,L_X$ and then reporting that $H,S,L$ reconstruct $\Phi$.
PETU therefore distinguishes:
\begin{enumerate}
\item **Internal closure reconstruction:** $\Phi_X$ is derived from structural features and must be labelled internal.
\item **Derived unity:** $U_X=(H_XS_XL_X)^{1/3}$ is a defined PETU index, not an independent endpoint.
\item **External endpoint prediction:** an independent endpoint $Y_X$ is not used in constructing $H,S,L,\Phi$.
\item **Fragility prediction:** $F_X$ is derived or external, and its construction must be stated.
\item **Natural-versus-perturbed discrimination:** class labels must not leak into feature construction.
\item **Anti-leakage validation:** grouped cross-validation, held-out domains, held-out sites or held-out time blocks are required where applicable.
\end{enumerate}

A strong confirmatory test should include at least one endpoint that is external to the construction of $H,S,L$.

## Perturbations, null models and falsification

Let $\pi_H,\pi_S,\pi_L$ be perturbation operators targeting support, structure or linkage.
For relevant disruptive perturbations, PETU predicts
\[
\Phi_X>\Phi_{\pi_i(X)}
\quad\text{or}\quad
F_{\pi_i(X)}>F_X,
\qquad i\in\{H,S,L\}.
\]
The perturbative response is
\[
\Delta_\Phi^i=\Phi_X-\Phi_{\pi_i(X)}.
\]

Null models may include label shuffling, feature shuffling, within-group permutation, block shuffling, degree-preserving graph randomization, phase randomization, spatially constrained nulls and matched-complexity random features.

The theory is weakened or falsified when:
\begin{enumerate}
\item independent $HSL\rightarrow\Phi$ fails systematically;
\item $HSL$ does not outperform $H$-only when $\Hraw$ is properly isolated;
\item perturbations fail to reduce $\Phi$ or increase fragility in predicted contexts;
\item alternative operationalizations erase the pattern;
\item cross-domain convergence depends on circular $\Phi$ definitions;
\item preregistered replication fails in anchor domains;
\item external endpoints fail under anti-leakage validation.
\end{enumerate}

## Multi-scale and dynamical closure

Let $r\in\mathcal{R}_X$ be an observational scale.
A scale-indexed triadic system is
\[
H_X(r),S_X(r),L_X(r),\Phi_X(r).
\]
Multi-scale closure occurs when
\[
\Phi_X(r)\approx\mathcal{F}_{X,r}\big(H_X(r),S_X(r),L_X(r)\big)
\]
across a declared set of scales.

For dynamic systems:
\[
H=H(t),\quad S=S(t),\quad L=L(t),\quad \Phi=\Phi(t).
\]
A general dynamical form is
\[
\frac{dH}{dt}=f_H(H,S,L,\theta_H),\quad
\frac{dS}{dt}=f_S(H,S,L,\theta_S),
\]
\[
\frac{dL}{dt}=f_L(H,S,L,\theta_L),\quad
\frac{d\Phi}{dt}=f_\Phi(H,S,L,\Phi,\theta_\Phi).
\]
Triadic dynamical closure predicts
\[
\Delta\Phi\approx\mathcal{D}(\Delta H,\Delta S,\Delta L).
\]

## Co-inherence and non-separability

The relation among $H,S,L$ can be represented by a co-inherence matrix:
\[
C_X=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&C_{HS}&C_{HL}\\
C_{SH}&1&C_{SL}\\
C_{LH}&C_{LS}&1
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
C_{ij}\in[0,1].
\]
The components are distinguishable but may not be separable.
A system contains irreducible triadic information when an interaction object is non-zero:
\[
I_{HSL}(X_T)\neq0.
\]
This may be operationalized by interaction regression terms, synergy measures, tensor contractions, topological invariants or model comparison.

## Categorical structural equivalence

Let $\Tri$ be the category of triadically structured systems.
An object is
\[
X_T=(X,H_X,S_X,L_X,\Phi_X).
\]
A morphism
\[
f:X_T\rightarrow Y_T
\]
maps observational units through $f_\Omega:\Omega_X\rightarrow\Omega_Y$ and preserves structural roles up to admissible distortion:
\[
H_Y(f_\Omega(\omega))\approx h_f(H_X(\omega)),
\]
and similarly for $S,L,\Phi$.

A closure map is
\[
\Theta_X:H_X\times S_X\times L_X\rightarrow \Phi_X.
\]
The categorical layer is a formal language for structural comparison, not an independent proof of PETU.
The measurable non-commutativity error is
\[
E_\Theta(f)=
\left\|
f_\Phi\circ\Theta_X-\Theta_Y\circ f_{HSL}
\right\|.
\]
A morphism is approximately closure-preserving when
\[
E_\Theta(f)<\tau_\Theta.
\]

## Tensorial and field-theoretic extension

For high-dimensional systems, scalar components can be generalized:
\[
H_i,\qquad S_{ij},\qquad L_{ijk}.
\]
A tensorial closure form is
\[
\Phi_X=\mathcal{C}_X[H_i,S_{ij},L_{ijk}],
\]
with a minimal contraction
\[
\Phi_X=
\sum_{i,j,k}W^{ijk}_XH_iS_{ij}L_{ijk}.
\]
In a field-theoretic direction, one may define fields over a manifold or observational space:
\[
H(x),\quad S(x),\quad L(x),\quad \Phi(x),
\]
and a local closure density
\[
\phi(x)=\mathcal{F}_x(H(x),S(x),L(x)).
\]
These extensions are mathematical research horizons; they are not yet established physical theories.

## Variational stability and phase transitions

A variational version can be written using a triadic action:
\[
\mathcal{A}_X[T]=
\int
\left(
E_\Phi(T)
+\lambda_U(1-U_X)
+\lambda_B(1-B_X)
+\lambda_DD_X^{gap}
+\lambda_RR_X
+V_T(T)
\right)\,dt.
\]
Coherent regimes are candidate low-action configurations:
\[
\delta\mathcal{A}_X=0.
\]
Fragility rises when the triadic potential increases:
\[
V_T\uparrow,\qquad F_X\rightarrow F_c,\qquad \Psi\downarrow.
\]
A phase transition may occur when
\[
\lambda_{\min}\left(\nabla_T^2V_T\right)=0,
\]
or when a small triadic displacement produces a large coherence shift:
\[
\|\Delta T\|<\varepsilon_T
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
|\Delta\Phi|>\tau_\Phi.
\]

## Law-candidate criteria

Triadic structural closure should be formulated as a law-candidate only over the admissible class $\Nstar$.
A system $X\in\Nstar$ satisfies strong triadic closure when:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $H_X,S_X,L_X,\Phi_X$ are measurable and normalized;
\item $\Phi_X\approx\mathcal{F}_X(H_X,S_X,L_X)$;
\item $P(HSL\rightarrow\Phi)>\tau_\Phi$;
\item $G_3^{diff}>\tau_G$, or apparent reducibility is explained by the $\Hraw/\Hstr$ distinction;
\item perturbations of $H,S,L$ reduce $\Phi$ or increase $F$;
\item the architecture survives independent validation or preregistered replication.
\end{enumerate}
This is a formal empirical criterion, not a purely deductive theorem.

## Limitations

The formal theory remains dependent on empirical operationalizations.
Cross-domain equivalence is structural, not mechanistic.
$\Phi$ must be protected against circular definition.
The $\Hraw/\Hstr$ distinction is essential and may not always be empirically resolvable.
Tensorial, categorical and variational extensions are proposed research horizons.
The present evidence supports a candidate structural principle, not an established universal law.
